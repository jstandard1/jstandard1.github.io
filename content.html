Business 101

If you’ll be soon heading on a business trip to Tokyo or elsewhere in Japan, this quick guide will walk you through what you should do BEFORE you leave and what to expect when you meet and interact with your Japanese hosts.

Bring a Gift

Gift giving is embedded in Japanese culture. Gifts do not need to be expensive, but will be appreciated as an indication you spent time to consider your hosts.

Here are a few tried and true examples:
  • Artisan chocolates. Avoid very sweet ones and also avoid mint chocolates which are not popular in Japan.
  • Snacks from Trader Joes. Goods from here have a great mix of interesting packaging, a distinctive brand, and are generally tasty.
  • Useful company swag such as notebooks or mugs.

Bowing Vs. Shaking Hands

Japanese do not typically shake hands with each other, but rather exchange bows. However, they generally know Western business people do and it is acceptable to shake hands

It is polite to bow to your hosts while keeping in mind these rules:
  • DO NOT put your hands in prayer position. NO ONE DOES THIS!
  • Don't bow too deeply. Generally an incline of the head is enough.

Business Cards

Meetings generally don’t begin until everyone in the room has finished exchanging their business cards. Typically the highest rank person exchanges cards first. Treat business cards as treasures, as if they are an extension of your hosts.

  • Don't put them in your clothes pockets. Place them on the desk during the meeting and then slip them into your briefcase or, ideally, your business card holder.
  • Do not write on them, it's rude!
There is a special ritual typically followed during the physical exchange of the cards, where you present them with 2 hands and transition to accepting your counterpart’s card with 2 hands. See this video for more information: Youtube Link

Meetings

Be on-time! Japanese are very punctual, often arriving 5-10 mins early to a meeting. Being late shows a lack of preparation and lack of respect for others’ time.

Pro Tip: To help remember names, arrange your business cards to match the seating arrangements.

Dress

Japan is generally conservative when it comes to business attire, particularly Tokyo. Men are expected to suit up!, including jackets and ties while avoiding jeans or casual clothes. Women should avoid above-the-knee skirts and wear stockings, even in humid summer weather.

Conversation

Make sure to speak extra slowly and clearly if you’re unsure about the English ability of those in the room. They will likely not ask you to slow down out of politeness or shyness to admit they do not understand. Japan has a non-confrontational culture and a communication style filled with non-verbal cues and subtleties. There are a few other common verbal cues to be aware of:

  • "Yes" does not always mean "Yes". In many cases it may simply mean acknowledgement of your statement. You may need to ask specific questions to clarify what the "Yes" actually means.
  • "It's difficult" likely means "No" or "We cannot/will not". It is not a statement about the difficulty of a task.
  • Be comfortable with silence. Silence is not nearly as terrifying to conversation in Japan as it is in Western countries where people may speak just to avoid an awkward lull in the conversation.

Transportation

Japan’s train system is equal parts magical and intimidating for first-timers. However, you’ll need to master the basics as other forms of transport are expensive (taxis) or even trickier to use (buses).

Trains

In Tokyo the most common train system you’ll take is called the “JR”, short for Japan Rail. There are 6 major JR lines in Tokyo.

Yamanote Line A loop around Tokyo. This will be your go-to train to get most major areas including Shibuya, Shinjuku, Tokyo Station, and Ueno. Trains come every few minutes on this line.

Shinkansen Line Bullet train which will take you to Kyoto, Osaka, Hiroshima, and (as of recently) Hokkaido. You need to buy tickets in advance, your Suica card doesn't work for this train.

Chuo Line (Rapid) Direct line across Tokyo. Good for getting to Tokyo Station or heading west to Kichijoji.

Chuo/Sobu Line (Local) A local service version of the Chuo line, stopping at every station.

Keihin Tohoku Line Access areas just north (Saitama) and south (Yokohama) of Tokyo.

Saikyo Line Runs along western edge of Yamanote and heads to Saitama.

Tickets

There are 2 main types of tickets for trains

Single-use Tickets
  • Generally used for short distances.
  • Purchased for a specific amount of money (ex. ¥280). The more you spend, the farther you can go.

How to use

  1. When entering the station, insert into the opening at the front of the ticket gate.
  2. The ticket will come back out. MAKE SURE TO TAKE IT!
  3. When exiting the station, insert the ticket as before. If you chose the right amount of money, the ticket will not come back out.
Suica (rechargeable card)

A credit card-sized plastic card which you add money to.

  • You add money to the card and just scan it as you enter/exit trains.
  • If you run low on money, just add more from a ticket machine.
  • 1000x more convenient than buying single tickets everywhere.
  • Can also be used to make purchases at most convenience stores and vending machines.

How to use

  1. When entering the station, just scan it at the front of the ticket gate.
  2. When exiting, do the same.

Where to buy

  • The JR office in Narita Airport (recommended as you receive a discount)
  • Ticket kiosks at most major train stations. (note: takes some setup time as you need to enter your name)

What if I have a problem or aren't sure how to go somewhere?

Ask a station attendant. They're inside a small room near the ticket gates.

Money Matters

Japan, despite its talking robots, still relies on cash for many daily transactions. Hotels, department stores, and large/upscale restaurants usually accept credit cards, but train stations, smaller shops, and restaurants typically do not.

Credit Cards

Mastercard (Cirrus) and Visa (Plus) credit cards are the only ones typically accepted. You’ll have a tougher time with Amex and other cards. It’s recommended to bring at least 2 cards, 1 Mastercard and 1 Visa.

Where to Get Cash

Foreign Exchange

Use the Foreign Exchange office at Narita or Haneda, you’ll get better rates than in your home country.

7-Eleven

They usually have ATMs which accept international cards and are open 24 hours a day.

Post Office

These are your next best bet, but not a guaranteed one. Most larger post offices will have international ATMs and will typically be open from 7AM - 10PM Mon-Fri, with reduced hours on weekends. Smaller ones are a gamble both in terms of ATMs and hours.

Department Stores

Large and upscale department stores are likely to have an international ATM. AEON department stores are one example.

Quick Tips

  • Check your card issuer’s fees for foreign transactions
  • Bring a small pouch to carry coins or you’ll soon be drowning in them

Etiquette

Eating

  • Chopsticks are the default, but most places do have forks available.
  • Use the back end of your chopsticks (the part you do not put in your mouth) to take food from commonal plates.
  • It’s not rude to slurp noodles, such as ramen. In fact, it’s quite commonplace.
  • People generally don’t eat while walking around. Which also means…
  • There are few public trashcans. To dispose of plastic bottles, look near vending machines for recycling containers with small, bottle-sized holes on them.
  • Tipping is not a custom. If you do, someone may run after you saying you forgot your money.
  • It’s common to receive your bill before you finish eating. Also, you’ll typically pay at a central register, rather than at your table.

Shoes

There are many occasions where you’ll need to take off your shoes, including: visiting someone’s house, in public baths or hot springs, and even in some restaurants.

When visiting someone’s house, you’ll typically take off your shoes in the entrance and in many cases put on a pair of slippers to use around the house.

  • Slip-on or easy to remove shoes are recommended.
  • Wear decent socks
  • NEVER wear shoes on tatami (thatched bamboo) mats
  • At someone’s house there is typically a pair of “Bathroom Only” slippers. DO NOT wear the normal house slippers into the bathroom.
